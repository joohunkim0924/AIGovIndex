How countries are positioned

This index uses a ternary positioning system — not a score or ranking. Every position reflects analytical judgment based on five measurable governance dimensions, triangulated against primary legislative sources and academic literature.

01
Legislative instrument
What type of governance instrument exists? Binding legislation with penalties → higher rights/sovereign score. Voluntary guidelines → higher market score.
02
Individual rights provisions
Does the framework include rights to explainability, human review, or contesting automated decisions? Strong provisions → higher rights position.
03
State content control
Does the framework require AI outputs to align with state values or national security priorities? State content requirements → higher sovereignty position.
04
Industry self-governance role
How central is voluntary industry self-governance vs. state regulation? Primary self-governance role → higher market position.
05
Enforcement capacity
Does a dedicated enforcement institution exist with technical capacity? Dot size encodes this independently — larger dots = stronger binding enforcement.
Important: Positions are analytical approximations based on publicly available governance documents. They reflect the dominant philosophical orientation of each framework — not a judgment of quality, effectiveness, or desirability. All positions updated to June 2026.
How it works

Methodology

Each jurisdiction is positioned on a ternary plot based on how strongly its AI governance framework pulls toward each of three philosophical poles. Dot size independently encodes binding enforcement strength.

01
Three governance philosophies
Rights-protective, market-oriented, and sovereignty-centered. Ternary coordinates sum to 1.0. No country is purely one model.
02
Two views
Triangle shows three-way tension simultaneously. Compass shows individual rights vs. state control against voluntary vs. binding law — more intuitive for general audiences.
03
Dot size = enforcement
Dot size encodes binding enforcement strength. Larger = stronger legally enforceable mechanisms with meaningful penalties.
04
Timeline 2016–2026
Drag the year slider to see how governance frameworks evolved. Crisis milestones appear at key years.
Key insight: EU and China are both large dots (strong binding enforcement) but at opposite corners. Binding enforcement and rights protection are independent dimensions — China uses binding law to enforce state control, the EU uses it to protect individual rights.
