Section 1

Overview & core formula

This index measures governance philosophy as expressed in binding primary legal documents and official policy instruments, not enforcement outcomes or regulatory effectiveness.
R_raw  = Σ(R1 … R10)    // sum of 10 Rights indicators, each scored 0, 1, or 2
M_raw = Σ(M1 … M10)    // sum of 10 Market indicators, each scored 0, 1, or 2
S_raw  = Σ(S1 … S10)    // sum of 10 Sovereign indicators, each scored 0, 1, or 2

Total = R_raw + M_raw + S_raw

r  = R_raw ÷ Total      // ternary rights coordinate
m = M_raw ÷ Total      // ternary market coordinate
s  = 1.00 − r − m      // ensures exact sum to 1.00
Precision. With 10 indicators per dimension scored 0–2, each raw score ranges from 0 to 20. The maximum total is 60, giving a minimum step size of 1/60 ≈ 0.017 after normalization. Coordinates are rounded to two decimal places. The honest uncertainty at that precision is ±0.02, corresponding to a single indicator being scored one level differently by another researcher applying the same rubric to the same document. All indicators are weighted equally to minimize researcher subjectivity.
Section 2

The three-level scoring scale

Every indicator uses the same scale, applied exclusively to primary governance documents — legislation, official regulatory guidance, executive orders, and international treaties.

0
Absent
No meaningful provision exists. Silence, vague aspiration, or rhetorical reference without operational content all score 0.
Japan: no binding right to explanation → R3 = 0
1
Soft / partial / sector-specific
Exists but is voluntary, limited to one sector, or binding without meaningful enforcement capacity.
UK: sector regulators issue AI transparency guidance → R3 = 1
2
Binding / comprehensive
A binding legal obligation across sectors, with real enforcement mechanism and meaningful penalties or powers.
EU AI Act: binding right to explanation for high-risk AI → R3 = 2

Section 6

Binding Enforcement Score (bind%)

The binding enforcement score is an independently assessed measure, ranging from 0 to 100, representing the degree to which a jurisdiction’s AI governance operates through formally enacted legal instruments with explicit enforcement mechanisms — as opposed to voluntary guidelines, soft law, or industry self-governance frameworks. It is assessed independently of the three-pole coordinates (r, m, s) and captures governance mechanism rather than governance philosophy: both the EU (rights-protective) and China (sovereignty-centered) score high on bind because both govern AI through formal law with penalties, despite sitting at opposite poles of the triangle.

Binding Enforcement Score — Derivation

The binding enforcement score (bind%) is derived directly from five of the thirty indicators that measure governance mechanism rather than governance philosophy. It is calculated as follows:

bind% = (R7 + R8 + (2 − M1) + (2 − M4) + (2 − M7)) ÷ 10 × 100

Where:

  • R7 = Independent enforcement body acting on behalf of individuals (0/1/2)
  • R8 = Meaningful financial penalties calibrated to harm or global turnover (0/1/2)
  • M1 = Primary governance mechanism is voluntary — inverted, so M1=0 contributes 2 to bind (0/1/2)
  • M4 = No binding financial penalties exist — inverted, so M4=0 contributes 2 to bind (0/1/2)
  • M7 = No dedicated enforcement body with AI sanctioning powers — inverted, so M7=0 contributes 2 to bind (0/1/2)

Maximum possible score: (2+2+2+2+2) ÷ 10 × 100 = 100%

Minimum possible score: 0%

This formula is mechanism-blind with respect to governance philosophy: a jurisdiction governed through binding rights-protective law (EU) and one governed through binding sovereignty-centered law (China) can both score high on bind, reflecting that both use formal law with enforcement mechanisms — even though they sit at opposite poles of the triangle. This separation of enforcement mechanism from governance philosophy is what makes the compass horizontal axis analytically independent of the vertical axis.

For sovereignty-centered jurisdictions where mandatory registration, content pre-approval, or state oversight requirements add additional binding elements not captured in R7/R8/M1/M4/M7, the bind score may be manually adjusted upward from the formula baseline and documented in the country’s scoring rationale.

The five tiers mapping bind% to dot size are:

  • 85–100%: Strong binding — large dot
  • 65–84%: Moderate-strong
  • 40–64%: Moderate
  • 20–39%: Partial
  • 0–19%: Voluntary — small dot
Tier bind% Definition Examples
Strong binding85–100%Comprehensive enacted AI or data law with independent enforcement body and meaningful financial penalties across most sectorsEU, South Korea, Germany, France
Moderate-strong65–84%Significant binding AI law with penalty mechanisms but incomplete sectoral coverage or enforcement capacity still being builtChina, Estonia, Hungary
Moderate40–64%Mix of binding instruments in some sectors plus voluntary frameworks; or binding law with limited penalty mechanismsRussia, Switzerland, Taiwan, US (with state laws)
Partial20–39%Primarily voluntary governance with some binding elements, or older binding data law not specifically covering AIUnited States (federal), UAE, Qatar, Brazil
Voluntary0–19%Governance through guidelines, principles, and industry self-governance with no AI-specific binding obligationsJapan, Singapore, OECD, UNESCO, Chile

The bind score determines dot size in both the triangle and compass views (five discrete sizes) and drives the horizontal axis of the compass projection (bx = bind/100, mapped to [−1, +1]).

Compass coordinates (rx, bx)

The Compass view uses a two-axis system. The vertical axis (rx) maps individual rights vs. state control. The horizontal axis (bx) maps voluntary vs. binding governance. These are derived independently from the ternary coordinates.

rx = (r − s) × 1.15    // capped at [−1, +1]. Positive = rights-oriented. Negative = state control.
bx = existing enforcement score    // derived from the bind score in each year's timeline entry

The 1.15 multiplier stretches the rx axis to better utilize the full compass range, since most countries have moderate (r−s) values. Countries with r=0.75 and s=0.04 get rx=(0.71)×1.15=0.82, placing them near the top of the compass without reaching the extreme.

Section 9

Known limitations

Advocacy bodies vs governance actors
OECD and UNESCO score market-oriented because their frameworks are voluntary — but their philosophy is rights-protective. The formula measures governance mechanism, not governance intent. These are noted as edge cases.
Intent vs enacted reality
Canada and Australia have strong rights traditions but lack binding AI law following legislative failures. The formula scores current enacted governance, not aspiration. Timeline data captures the trajectory.
Inter-rater reliability
Indicator scoring requires reading primary documents. Two researchers may score a single indicator ±1, producing a coordinate shift of approximately ±0.02. This is the irreducible uncertainty of any document-based governance index.
Equal indicator weighting
All 10 indicators per dimension are weighted equally. A future version could weight foundational indicators (R1, S1) more heavily. Current equal weighting is a deliberate choice to minimize researcher subjectivity.
Section 10

Primary source basis

All indicator scores are based exclusively on the following primary source types. Secondary commentary, news articles, and think-tank summaries were not used as scoring inputs.

  • National AI legislation and regulations (official government portals and official journals)
  • Executive orders, cabinet decisions, and prime ministerial directives
  • Binding regulatory guidance issued by national supervisory authorities (e.g., CAC, BfDI, ICO, CNIL)
  • Constitutional provisions and constitutional court judgments
  • International treaties and intergovernmental agreements
  • National AI strategies where adopted by formal government decision (not consultation documents)
  • OECD AI Policy Observatory database for cross-verification
  • Stanford HAI AI Index 2026 for secondary validation of key facts
Position ≠ ranking. The formula produces a location in three-dimensional governance space, not a score from good to bad. A country positioned near the sovereignty corner is not worse than one near the rights corner — it reflects a different stated philosophy about what AI governance is for, as expressed in primary legal documents. The formula makes that philosophy visible and contestable, which is the point.
